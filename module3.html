Module 3: Spotting Fake News & Deepfakes

Learn to identify false information online

What is Fake News?

Fake news refers to false or misleading information that is presented as if it were real news. It's often designed to influence people's opinions or generate clicks and shares.

Common Types of Fake News:

  • Completely False Stories: Stories that are entirely made up
  • Misleading Headlines: Headlines that exaggerate or distort what's actually in the article
  • Opinion Presented as Fact: When someone's personal view is presented as if it were objective truth
  • Old News as Current: Sharing past events as if they just happened
  • Manipulated Images: Photos that have been altered to change their meaning

What are Deepfakes?

Deepfakes are videos, images, or audio recordings that have been digitally altered using artificial intelligence (AI) to make them look or sound like someone else, often in a very convincing way.

Why Deepfakes are Concerning:

  • They can make it appear that someone said or did something they never actually said or did
  • They can be used to spread false information or damage someone's reputation
  • They're becoming increasingly difficult to distinguish from real content

How Fake News Spreads

False information often travels faster and reaches more people than accurate information. This happens because:

  • Shocking or surprising stories tend to get more attention
  • Social media platforms make it easy to share content with just one click
  • Many people share articles based only on the headline, without reading the full story
  • Some websites create fake news specifically to generate advertising revenue from clicks

Tools for Verifying Stories

When you encounter news or images that seem suspicious, here are some tools to help verify them:

Fact-Checking Resources:

  • Snopes (snopes.com): A website dedicated to fact-checking rumors, news stories, and internet misinformation
  • FactCheck.org: A non-partisan website that monitors the factual accuracy of political claims
  • Reuters Fact Check: Fact-checking service from the global news organization
  • Google Reverse Image Search: Upload an image to see where else it appears online (helps identify if an image is old or taken out of context)

How to Use Google to Fact-Check:

  1. Open Google.com in your web browser
  2. Type in key details from the story along with words like "fact check" or "hoax"
  3. Look for results from reputable news sources or fact-checking websites

Tips for Spotting Fake Content

  • Check the source: Is it from a reputable news organization? Does the website look professional?
  • Look for unusual web addresses: Fake news sites often use addresses that sound like real news sources but with slight differences
  • Check the date: Sometimes old stories are reshared as if they're new
  • Read beyond the headline: Headlines can be misleading; read the full article
  • Look for multiple sources: If a story is true, multiple reputable news sources will likely report it
  • Check your emotional reaction: If a story makes you feel very angry or surprised, be extra careful to verify it
  • Look for unusual features in videos: Deepfakes often have unnatural eye movements, strange skin tones, or audio that doesn't quite match lip movements

Key Takeaways

  • Be skeptical of emotional or surprising headlines
  • Check the source and publication date
  • Use fact-checking websites when you're unsure
  • Be aware that videos and images can now be manipulated very convincingly
  • If something seems too good (or bad) to be true, it probably is
