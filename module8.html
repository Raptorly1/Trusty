Module 8: Reporting & Getting Help

What to do if you encounter a scam or become a victim

Where and How to Report Scams

If you encounter a scam or suspect you've been targeted, it's important to report it. Your report can help authorities track down scammers and prevent others from becoming victims.

Where to Report Different Types of Scams:

  • Federal Trade Commission (FTC): Report most types of scams at ReportFraud.ftc.gov or call 1-877-382-4357
  • FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3): Report internet crimes at www.ic3.gov
  • Social Security Administration: Report Social Security scams at oig.ssa.gov or call 1-800-269-0271
  • IRS: Report tax scams at www.treasury.gov/tigta/contact_report_scam.shtml
  • Local Police: Report scams that resulted in significant financial loss to your local police department

When reporting a scam, try to provide as much detail as possible, including:

  • Date and time of the contact
  • How the scammer contacted you (phone, email, text, etc.)
  • What information they requested
  • Any phone numbers, email addresses, or websites involved
  • Any money that was lost and how it was paid

How to Freeze Your Credit or Flag Identity Theft

If you suspect your personal information has been compromised, or you want to protect yourself proactively, freezing your credit is an important step.

What is a Credit Freeze?

A credit freeze prevents lenders from accessing your credit report, making it nearly impossible for identity thieves to open new accounts in your name. It's free to freeze and unfreeze your credit.

How to Freeze Your Credit:

You need to contact each of the three major credit bureaus separately:

  • Equifax: www.equifax.com/personal/credit-report-services/credit-freeze/ or call 800-685-1111
  • Experian: www.experian.com/freeze/center.html or call 888-397-3742
  • TransUnion: www.transunion.com/credit-freeze or call 888-909-8872

If You're a Victim of Identity Theft:

  1. Report it to the FTC at IdentityTheft.gov
  2. Follow their personalized recovery plan, which may include:
    • Placing fraud alerts on your credit reports
    • Reviewing your credit reports for unauthorized accounts
    • Disputing fraudulent charges
    • Creating an Identity Theft Report

Support Resources for Seniors

There are many organizations dedicated to helping seniors stay safe online and recover from scams:

Helpful Resources:

  • AARP Fraud Resource Center: www.aarp.org/money/scams-fraud/ or call 1-877-908-3360
  • Elder Fraud Hotline: 1-833-FRAUD-11 (1-833-372-8311)
  • National Council on Aging: www.ncoa.org/economic-security/money-management/scams-security/
  • Medicare Fraud: 1-800-MEDICARE (1-800-633-4227)
  • Tech Support: Many libraries offer free tech help for seniors
  • Local Senior Centers: Often provide resources and educational workshops

Remember that asking for help is a sign of wisdom, not weakness. There are people who want to help you navigate technology safely.

Sharing What You've Learned

One of the best ways to protect yourself and others is to share what you've learned about digital safety. When more people know how to recognize scams, it becomes harder for scammers to succeed.

Ways to Share Your Knowledge:

  • Tell friends and family about scams you've encountered or heard about
  • Share articles and resources about online safety on social media
  • If you hear someone mention something that sounds like a scam, gently share your concerns
  • Encourage friends to take this Trusty Digital Safety Course
  • Help others set up security features on their devices

By spreading awareness, you become part of the solution in making the internet safer for everyone.

Key Takeaways

  • Report scams to the appropriate authorities like the FTC, FBI, or local police
  • Consider freezing your credit reports to prevent identity theft
  • Use support resources designed specifically for seniors
  • Share what you've learned with friends and family to help protect them too
  • Remember that you're not alone—there are many resources available to help
